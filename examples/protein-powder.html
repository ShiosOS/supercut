How we picked these ads

Advertisers kill losing ads fast, so an ad still delivering after a month has earned a look. We searched 216 ads in this market — including its heaviest spenders, so the pool reflects where real budgets go, not just what got views — and kept the ones running 30+ days and seen in the last 30. An AI watched each surviving ad and answered a fixed set of questions about it; code then counted the answers. 5 ads that turned out to sell something else were rejected — they are listed at the bottom. No single ad is called a winner here. The findings are patterns that repeat across 57 ads from 41 different brands.

What to test next

The Fast-Paced Curiosity Hook

Hypothesis
If we use a curiosity tease hook with fast pacing, we expect higher click-through rates.
Why this test
Only 2 ads out of 57 currently use a curiosity tease, and fast pacing is successful in lifestyle montages (7 out of 8).
Call it on
CTR
Shot notes
  • Start with a close-up of a container without showing the label.
  • Use 3 cuts in the first 3 seconds.
  • End with a creator finally revealing the brand.
Risk
The viewer might scroll away if the product benefit isn't clear enough.
Draft script
You won't believe what's actually in my morning shake. Most people think it's just fruit, but it's this. One scoop changes everything. Check it out.

Hard CTA Professional Demo

Hypothesis
If we use a hard call to action inside a product demo, we expect higher conversion rates.
Why this test
Only 5 out of 11 product demos currently use a hard CTA, despite it being common in other formats.
Call it on
CVR
Shot notes
  • Show powder dissolving instantly in water.
  • Keep the lighting bright and professional.
  • Add an 'Order Now' text overlay at the very end.
Risk
It might feel too much like a traditional commercial for social media users.
Draft script
Stop drinking clumpy shakes. This powder mixes in five seconds with zero grit. Pure 25g of protein every time. Buy yours now.

Sincere Creator Problem-Solver

Hypothesis
If we use a sincere emotional tone in a problem-solution format, we can lower cost-per-acquisition.
Why this test
Only 3 out of 19 problem-solution ads use a sincere tone, while 13 lean on high energy.
Call it on
CPA
Shot notes
  • Creator speaks directly to the camera in a kitchen.
  • Use natural, soft lighting.
  • Highlight the struggle of finding a powder that doesn't cause bloating.
Risk
The video might lack the 'stop' power needed for a busy social feed.
Draft script
I honestly gave up on protein for a while because my stomach couldn't handle it. Then I found this. No bloating, just clean ingredients. It really helped me.

How reliable was the watching?

We measured the editing pace of every ad twice: once by the AI's eye, once by scene-detection software. The AI was off by 2.4 cuts on average, and within 2 cuts of the software on 60% of ads. The cut counts printed in the chapters are the software's, not the AI's.

Where a spoken hook could be checked against the ad library's own transcript (19 ads), the AI's quote matched 18 times and disagreed 1 time (ad f2acbfe8687de138601ee). The other 38 ads had on-screen-text hooks, no hook, or no transcript — nothing to check against.

10 of 57 ads were described by the AI as making no ask, yet the ad unit itself carries a purchase button (ads f66d9c73952b213626aca, f74632f1d06cdd6c45f57, fdc8b6f3ddcc4216a9cbb, f42800576e2de20271fa0, ff0b38aa61bfbb3e935db, b13c9ab4748e89cc7abb, f7abe59147eeb4aa56ab2, afc8513033f430404a49, f2e5596406d147a82051, 295504454ec3858df029). Read those "no ask" counts with that in mind: the AI judges the video, not the button under it.

Assumptions this report makes

  • "Still delivering" means the ad was seen in the ad library within the last 30 days. The data offers no direct "currently live" flag.
  • Days-running and play counts come from the ad library and are taken at face value.
  • A long-running ad earned study, but nothing here proves any single ad was profitable. Only the repeated patterns are conclusions.
  • Spoken quotes were transcribed by the AI from the audio; where the ad library had its own transcript, we cross-checked (see above).

Study ranking: ads are ordered for the example lists by a score of longevity ×0.5 + engagement ×0.3 + formatConfidence ×0.2, each part normalized within this pool, with at most one ad per brand in any list. Engagement is measured as a rate — likes and shares per play, a share counting 5× a like — because plays on a paid ad are bought, so raw counts would mostly rank budgets. Rates from under 10,000 plays are ignored as noise.
