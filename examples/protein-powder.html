How we picked these ads

Advertisers kill losing ads fast, so an ad still delivering after a month has earned a look. We searched 216 ads in this market — including its heaviest spenders, so the pool reflects where real budgets go, not just what got views — and kept the ones running 30+ days and seen in the last 30. An AI watched each surviving ad and answered a fixed set of questions about it; code then counted the answers. 2 ads that turned out to sell something else were rejected — they are listed at the bottom. No single ad is called a winner here. The findings are patterns that repeat across 54 ads from 39 different brands.

What to test next

Humor vs. Energy in UGC

Hypothesis
If we use a humorous tone in problem-solution videos, we can stand out from the energetic norm.
Why this test
While 15 ads use an energetic tone, only 1 ad in the UGC group uses humor.
Call it on
Click-Through Rate
Shot notes
  • Creator tries to open a difficult protein bag
  • Exaggerated face after tasting bad competing powder
  • Funny dance after mixing the new powder
  • Focus on relatability over fitness perfection
Risk
The humor might distract from the product benefits.
Draft script
Every protein I buy tastes like chalk. [Beat: Creator spits out dry powder] I finally found one that doesn't suck. It mixes in seconds and tastes like dessert. Stop punishing your taste buds. Shop now.

Late Product Reveal Test

Hypothesis
If we delay the product reveal in a montage, we may increase hook rate through curiosity.
Why this test
43 ads show the product in the first 3 seconds, while only 1 lifestyle ad shows it later.
Call it on
Average Watch Time
Shot notes
  • First 5 seconds focus only on a intense workout
  • Sweat dripping and heavy lifting shots
  • Product only appears as the reward at the end
  • Fast cuts between gym equipment
Risk
Viewers may scroll past if they don't know what is being sold.
Draft script
The work happens here. [Beat: Fast gym cuts] The recovery happens here. [Beat: Product reveal] Fuel your progress with the cleanest isolate. View now.

Question Hook Test in Testimonials

Hypothesis
If we start a creator testimonial with a direct question, we will improve engagement over bold claims.
Why this test
Only 1 creator testimonial used a question hook style, compared to 4 using bold claims.
Call it on
Engagement Rate
Shot notes
  • Creator looks directly at camera in a kitchen
  • Hook text overlay asks a specific diet question
  • Creator shows the powder bag immediately after the question
  • Natural, unpolished lighting
Risk
Questions can sometimes feel like generic clickbait.
Draft script
Are you actually getting enough protein to see muscle growth? I wasn't, until I added this to my morning coffee. It has 25g of protein and zero sugar. Check it out today.

What to double-check

Everything above comes from an AI watching ads one at a time and code counting its answers. Here is where that process was noisy, and what it threw out.

Ads rejected as off-market (2)

The search also returned ads selling something else. They were dropped before any counting, so they do not touch the numbers above.

AdvertiserWhy it was dropped
Amy Jooff-market: sells "Clio Greek Yogurt Bars, a chocolate-covered high-protein snack."
Fitness Plugoff-market: sells "A stimulant-free pre-workout formula designed to increase pumps, power, and endurance."

How reliable was the watching?

We measured the editing pace of every ad twice: once by the AI's eye, once by scene-detection software. The AI was off by 2.1 cuts on average, and within 2 cuts of the software on 63% of ads. The cut counts printed in the chapters are the software's, not the AI's.

Where a spoken hook could be checked against the ad library's own transcript (18 ads), the AI's quote matched 17 times and disagreed 1 time (ad f2acbfe8687de138601ee). The other 36 ads had on-screen-text hooks, no hook, or no transcript — nothing to check against.

8 of 54 ads were described by the AI as making no ask, yet the ad unit itself carries a purchase button (ads f66d9c73952b213626aca, f74632f1d06cdd6c45f57, fdc8b6f3ddcc4216a9cbb, f42800576e2de20271fa0, ff0b38aa61bfbb3e935db, b13c9ab4748e89cc7abb, f7abe59147eeb4aa56ab2, afc8513033f430404a49). Read those "no ask" counts with that in mind: the AI judges the video, not the button under it.

Assumptions this report makes

  • "Still delivering" means the ad was seen in the ad library within the last 30 days. The data offers no direct "currently live" flag.
  • Days-running and play counts come from the ad library and are taken at face value.
  • A long-running ad earned study, but nothing here proves any single ad was profitable. Only the repeated patterns are conclusions.
  • Spoken quotes were transcribed by the AI from the audio; where the ad library had its own transcript, we cross-checked (see above).

Study ranking: ads are ordered for the example lists by a score of longevity ×0.5 + engagement ×0.3 + formatConfidence ×0.2, each part normalized within this pool, with at most one ad per brand in any list. Engagement is measured as a rate — likes and shares per play, a share counting 5× a like — because plays on a paid ad are bought, so raw counts would mostly rank budgets. Rates from under 10,000 plays are ignored as noise.
