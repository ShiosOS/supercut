Background

The problem this product exists for

Creative strategists and founders who sell physical products on paid social face a familiar trap. The ad library is full of noise. Engagement sorts reward big budgets and viral accidents. “Winning ads” lists are opaque. And if you hand a pile of creatives to a large language model and ask “what’s working?”, you get confident prose that cannot be audited — numbers with no receipts, patterns invented across ads the model was never asked to measure.

Supercut’s answer is a single document: a self-contained HTML playbook for one market (coffee, protein powder, …). The document shows format chapters with counted patterns, hook-frame strips pulled from the ads themselves, and three tests to run next — each with a draft script. It is built for someone who needs to brief a shoot tomorrow, not someone who wants a dashboard.

The product thesis is deliberately narrow. Longevity is an admission filter, not a verdict. Advertisers kill losing creative quickly, so an ad that has been delivering for 30+ days and was seen in the last 30 has earned study. The search also includes the market’s heaviest spenders, so “still delivering” means real money kept backing the ad — not merely that it lingered. No single ad is ever called a winner. Conclusions come only from counting regularities across the admitted pool (“34 of 39 ads show the product in the first 3 seconds”).

Skip ahead if you already know the domain. The rest of this background is the narrow architecture you must own in the interview: the one rule that keeps AI from inventing market claims, and the stage boundary that makes every number reproducible from files on disk.

The architectural rule of the house

The entire codebase is organized around one sentence, restated in docs/architecture.md and enforced by module boundaries:

The one rule AI describes single ads. Code finds the patterns. AI explains the patterns. The video model never sees two ads at once, so it cannot invent market-level claims. The prose model never sees a video — only counted tallies — so every claim it makes has a count behind it. Everything in between is deterministic TypeScript.

That rule is not a slogan. It dictates file ownership. describe.ts may fill a fixed questionnaire for one ad. tally.ts may count answers and attach ad ids as receipts. explain.ts may phrase those counts into chapter prose. Cross the line — hand all the ads to one big “find the patterns” prompt — and the playbook becomes un-auditable. The three-stage cost in code is the price of numbers you can defend.

System layers. Provider quirks are quarantined; AI is constrained to schema-validated edges; the strategist-facing surface is deliberately thin.

Tech stack, stated plainly: Bun for the pipeline, TypeScript strict with noUncheckedIndexedAccess, Zod v4 as the LLM contract / runtime validator / static type (one schema does all three), Next.js 16 + React 19 for the front door, ffmpeg on PATH for mechanical video facts. Deterministic modules are unit-tested with bun test; model calls and live API pulls are not mocked into fake confidence — they are schema-validated and measured in the playbook appendix.

Data flow. Notice the two AI boxes are isolated: one never sees two ads; the other never sees a video.

Suppose after gate we have 12 oat-milk ads. Tally finds that 9 of 12 put the product on screen in the first 3 seconds. That count object looks like: 

{
  "label": "first 3 seconds",
  "count": 9,
  "adIds": ["a1","a2","a3","a4","a5","a6","a7","a8","a9"]
}

The explain model receives that structure (plus the rest of the tally and a QA report). It is allowed to write: “Show the product in the first three seconds (9 of 12).” It is not allowed to invent “most ads use a yellow logo” — that number does not exist in the tallies. Schema validation and prompting reinforce the constraint; the deeper guarantee is informational: the model simply never receives the videos.

Where trust comes from

Trust in this system is not “we used a good model.” It is a stack of complementary ground truths:

  • Admission trust: survival + recency + spend stratum — money kept paying.
  • Description trust: one ad, fixed schema, coarse time bins (model timestamps drift 2–5s).
  • Mechanical trust: cut counts and hook frames from ffmpeg, not the model.
  • Pattern trust: deterministic tallies with ad-id receipts.
  • Honesty trust: appendix publishes rejections, skips, cut MAE, quote corroboration, CTA discrepancies.
Edge case interviewers love PipiSpy has no live flag. “Still delivering” is a stated proxy: last_put_time within 30 days. Survival says the ad kept earning spend, not that it was profitable. Dead ≠ failed (campaigns end). That is why winners-vs-losers comparison was scoped out — the trustworthy half of the signal is longevity-as-filter, not death-as-failure.

Why engagement is a rate

Plays on paid ads are bought. Ranking on raw play counts crowns the biggest budgets. The study score therefore uses (likes + shares × 5) / plays, with a 10,000-play floor so 180 likes on 200 plays cannot beat the world. Shares weigh 5× likes because passing a creative on is a deliberate endorsement; a like is a tap. Longevity still leads the weighted sum (0.5 / 0.3 / 0.2 with format confidence), because longevity is the admission thesis. The formula is printed in every playbook footnote so ranking stays inspectable.

Ingest, triage, watch

findAds — six biases, one budget

PipiSpy charges 1 credit per result. Failures often return HTTP 200 with success: false, so the client checks the body. sort_type must be the string "desc". Every raw response is cached under data/<m>/searches/<paramsHash>.json. The recency cutoff rounds to the UTC day so same-day re-runs hit cache.

Four sort strata on the broad keyword (engagement, delivery days, last found, ad spend) plus two AI-suggested sub-keyword pulls (keyword type 5) at 40 each = 240 searched ads, exactly MAX_ADS_SEARCHED. For consumer-product markets, data_type: [3] and is_product: true cut games and apps upstream — recon showed ~60% of credits otherwise went to ads the gate would drop. The flag is one constant (CONSUMER_PRODUCT_MARKET), not a provider abstraction: an app market would flip to is_app by hand. 

// findAds.ts — admission restated in code even though the API also filters.
// Cached responses and provider drift mean you never trust a boundary you can re-check.
function passesAdmission(ad: Ad, lastSeenAfter: number): boolean {
  return ad.daysRunning >= MIN_DAYS_RUNNING && ad.lastSeenAt >= lastSeenAfter;
}

triage — optimization, not authority

First coffee run: 56 watched, 42 rejected, 14 relevant — thin pool. Caption triage (chunks of 50, four parallel calls) sorts likely-on-market first and roughly tripled yield of the same 70-video budget. If a chunk fails, that chunk stays “unsure.” Triage never rejects.

watchAd — one download window, then delete

withVideo downloads via curl (Bun’s fetch hung on the CDN; --max-time 120 guarantees a stuck download skips one ad). In that window: probe duration, size check (40MB — video goes base64 to the model), average-hash fingerprint of the middle 8×8 gray frame, ffmpeg scene cuts in the first 10s, four hook frames as ~320px webp in one ffmpeg pass, then describeAd. Video deleted in finally.

Concurrency hazard you must know Watches run 32-wide. Mechanicals are an in-memory Map loaded once and persisted once after the phase. Thirty-two tasks doing read-modify-write on one JSON file would corrupt it; thirty-two mutations on one Map on a single-threaded event loop cannot. Progress counters increment in JS between awaits — acceptable for a status bar, not a ledger.

The FactSheet questionnaire forces coarse bins for visual timing (first 3 seconds | 3 to 10 seconds | later | never) because the model’s event timestamps drift. It asks marketFit so the gate has something deterministic to decide on. estimatedCutsFirst10s is kept only for QA against ffmpeg — chapters print the measured cuts.

Dedupe, gate, tally, QA

dedupeCreatives

Same creative often runs under several ad ids and page names. Sort by longevity descending; keep a candidate unless an already-kept fingerprint matches: identical sha256, or duration within 1.5s and perceptual hash Hamming distance ≤ 5 bits of 64. Then capPerBrand(..., 4) again prefers longest-running.

gate

Ten lines that earn their keep. Recon: top engagement result for “coffee” was a lip-balm ad whose transcript mentioned coffee. Adjacent products stay (segments can break them out); off-market is rejected with off-market: sells "…" into the appendix.

tally

Pure functions. Formats below MIN_ADS_FOR_CHAPTER (3) go to smallFormats. Thin chapters (<5) still render but with a banner. Distributions sort by count; video length buckets sort shortest-to-longest. Facebook often lacks duration/plays — those ads drop out of the affected denominator so “N of M” stays honest. Pool size < 10 refuses to write a playbook at all (“counting regularities in a handful of ads is astrology”).

qa

Mean absolute error of model cut estimates vs ffmpeg; share within 2 cuts; low-confidence format ids; spoken-hook word-overlap ≥ 70% against provider ASR (on-screen text hooks are not checkable — ASR can’t see them); CTA discrepancy when model says ctaStyle: "none" but button matches /shop|buy|order|purchase|cart/i (“Learn more” is consistent with soft/no ask).

Explain, brief, render, app

explain + exemplars in parallel

explainTally sends { tally, qa } only. Schema demands counted lines with count/total copied from tallies, one chapter per format label in order, exactly three tests. While that call flies, buildExemplarsByFormat scores admitted members (excluding low-confidence labels), picks ≤3 with at most one brand each, embeds frame data URLs, and fetches detail permalinks only for those exemplars — recon showed ad_fee was play_count/1000 on 30/33 samples, so “estimated spend” is never printed.

render

String-assembled HTML with inlined CSS and base64 webps — readable from file://, email, or print. Numbers come from Tally; prose sits beside counts. Rare segment labels (<2 ads) bundle into “N one-off segments.” Coffee example: ~0.9MB, 58 ads / 45 brands, product-demo chapter entirely on Facebook in that snapshot.

app

POST /api/scan spawns bun run src/scan.ts <slug> detached and unrefs; progress is a JSON file polled every 3s. If a scan is already running for that market, return the slug and watch it. Delete refuses while running (409). marketSlug strips to [a-z0-9-] so paths cannot escape data/ or examples/. Briefs read tallies.json and rewrite the three tests for one brand — still tally-bound.

Decisions you should be ready to defend

These are the answers that separate “I built a pipeline” from “I made product and epistemology choices.” Practice saying them in your own words.

  1. Why not one big “find patterns” prompt? Fast to build, impossible to audit, invents market claims. Three stages cost code; every number becomes reproducible from questionnaires on disk.
  2. Why longevity, not plays? Plays measure budget. Survival is what advertisers pay for with their own money. Combined with the spend sort stratum: “ran a long time with real money behind it.” Still: conclusions only from pool regularities.
  3. Why keep the gate after API pre-filters? Pre-filters remove games/apps; the gate still catches e-commerce that sells the wrong product (coffee tables in a coffee scan). Defense in depth; noise becomes a measured appendix row instead of silent contamination.
  4. Why no zombie cap on delivery days? A 900-day survivor seen in the last 30 is what’s working now — it just started long ago. Median days live per chapter makes veteran weight visible.
  5. Why cut Meta reach / advertiser-anchored scans? Verified against the API, then cut. Reach is a second data model that won’t compare cleanly with play-count rows inside one document. Advertiser spine is a different product (competitor intel). One consistent weaker metric beats two inconsistent strong ones.
  6. Why not a whitespace finder? “Nobody does X” needs census coverage; a gap can mean “tried and died.” The playbook may observe rarity with a count; it does not advise charging into a gap.
  7. Why transient videos? Not ours to store; no analytical gain once frames, mechanicals, and fact sheets exist. Derived artifacts are small and versioned by schema/threshold.
  8. Why a thin app? One document a strategist can act on beats a chat + scoring dashboard + asset generator half-done. Progress-via-file is enough for one scan per market.
